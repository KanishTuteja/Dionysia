II. The Founding Charter

Every platform pretends to have values. Most discover them under pressure and rewrite them quietly. DIONYSIA's charter is fixed, in writing, before the doors open. It is the rail every editorial, product, and infrastructure decision will pass through — at this size, and at every size after.

— We believe —
  • that AI is a medium, the way 16mm or DV is a medium — not a subject, not a genre, and never the noun in our sentences.
  • that the films are directed, not summoned. A tool can render a frame. Only a filmmaker can intend one.
  • that emotional resonance is the only criterion that matters. Audiences will always forgive imperfect images. They never forgive emotional disconnection.
  • that a serious work deserves to land in a place where serious people will actually watch it — credits, breath, silence, and all.
  • that curation is care, not gatekeeping. A "no" with a reason is more respectful than an algorithm's indifference.
  • that the filmmakers who built the first chapter should be honored as such — economically, editorially, and culturally — for as long as the platform exists.
— We reject —
  • the contextual collapse of feeds — where a year of work shares a scroll with an "animals dancing" clip and authorship quietly disappears.
  • the slot machine of unmoderated upload, the cynicism of "creator-owned" as marketing copy without real ownership, and any phrase that uses the word "content" to describe a film.
  • open submissions without an editorial wall. Submission is not publication.
  • algorithmic for-you feeds, where popularity becomes a substitute for taste and curation disappears into noise. Discovery on DIONYSIA stays editorially intentional — at this catalog size, and at every size after.
  • any framing — internal or external — that treats founding filmmakers as a stepping stone, a stage, or an audience to be replaced. You are not the rollout. You are the institution.

  5. Honesty, in private. Tell us what is broken. Tell us what is missing. Tell us when the editorial framing is wrong about your work. The founding circle is a peerage, not a customer base — and the only way the platform stays serious as it scales is if the people who built the first chapter speak honestly to the person running it. The tool-side perspective you carry — six platforms in, daily — is one of the registers this conversation needs the most.
— What we do NOT ask —
  1. No exclusivity required. At this stage, no exclusivity is asked of you, on any film. You can keep distributing wherever you already do — your social, your client deliverables, the tool partners' channels when they feature your work, festivals, anywhere. As the platform matures, opt-in premiere-window agreements with clear, transparent terms will be introduced — never as a default condition, and never tied to founding-circle status. The regulations that govern licensing, windowing, and rights will be written carefully and shared in writing before they take effect.
  2. No volume targets, no upload cadence. We will never ask you to ship faster. The founding circle exists in the opposite direction from those incentives. If you bring one film or you bring several, both are welcomed on the terms you set.
  3. No marketing labor. You are not a growth channel for the platform. We will not ask you to repost, cross-promote, tag, or perform on social. If you choose to share through your own channels or your educator cohorts, that is yours.
  4. No silence in exchange. If at any point this stops being a place you want your work in, you tell us, we lift the films, and you leave with everything you came with. The door swings both ways, at every size the platform reaches.
IV. Submission Specifications

The platform runs on a premium video pipeline (Mux), the same infrastructure used by MasterClass, Patreon, and HBO Max. The specs below will read as familiar — they are, deliberately, the language a photographer-trained AI filmmaker working with India's commercial pipeline already speaks.

  • Master format ProRes 422 HQ, DNxHR HQ, or high-bitrate H.264 / H.265 .mp4. Any commercial-grade master is fine; we transcode from there.
  • Resolution 1920×1080 minimum; 3840×2160 (4K) preferred where it exists. Vertical orientations supported but framed for letterbox display by default.
  • Frame rate Native to your project — 23.976, 24, 25, or 29.97 fps. Do not conform unnecessarily.
  • Audio Stereo or 5.1, 48 kHz, −14 LUFS integrated target (broadcast-streaming standard). Loudness pass strongly preferred; we can normalize if needed.
  • Runtime No upper or lower bound. Shorts (under 15 minutes), longer pieces, branded films, experimental frames — the editorial frame adapts. We resist the dogma that any one runtime is "right" for the medium.
  • Poster A title-card still at 800×1200 (vertical) plus a 1920×1080 hero frame. Given your photography background, your own key art will almost certainly be the better choice than anything we'd cut.
  • Captions .vtt or .srt — English required. Hindi as a second track if the film lives in that register. Machine-generated draft is fine; your final approval.
  • Provenance Tools used (Runway, Pika, Higgsfield, Weavy, Caimera, Dreamina — whichever fired on the piece), hand-authored layers, AI-generated layers, and — if any synthesized likeness appears — performer-consent attestation. Part of the public film record. A moat, not a footnote.
  • Delivery Direct link to a private cloud transfer (Frame.io, Dropbox, Google Drive, WeTransfer Pro). No public file hosts. Receipt confirmed in writing inside twenty-four hours.

These specifications are the current pipeline. The platform's video, audio, and rendering quality will continue to improve with every update — and masters delivered into the founding library today automatically inherit every upgrade after. The standard floor will only rise. Yours rises with it.

If any of the above is a problem — gear constraints, formats from a client master, anything — write back. The specs are a target, not a wall.
